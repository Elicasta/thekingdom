Recap

What the youth just heard

Solomon knew God. He experienced God, prayed to God, and carried wisdom from God. Yet knowing God at one point did not protect him from drifting later.

His fall did not begin in one dramatic moment. It formed through decisions that slowly moved God out of the center. His relationships affected his worship. What he tolerated altered what he loved. What he permitted around the throne affected who ruled his heart.

The question is not whether we have been near God. The question is whether God presently rules us.
Joshua 24:15 · KJV

“Choose you this day whom ye will serve.”

  • Drift happens by decisions, not only by moments.
  • What is tolerated can become what is loved.
  • Inherited exposure must become personal surrender.
  • A throne decision never remains only personal. It travels.

Section One

Ignored warnings

2 Chronicles 36:15–16 · KJV

“And the LORD God of their fathers sent to them by his messengers… because he had compassion on his people… till there was no remedy.”

Jerusalem did not fall because God became angry without warning. Before Babylon arrived, God spoke. Before the city was surrounded, God spoke. Before the temple burned, God spoke.

The warnings were not evidence that God wanted destruction. They were evidence that God wanted to rescue His people from it. Conviction is exposure for rescue. God reveals the destination of a road while we are still choosing whether to walk it.

The people did not always reject God with a public declaration. Their rejection became visible in the repeated habit of hearing without obeying. A person can become practiced at feeling conviction, waiting for the feeling to pass, then leaving unchanged.

Not answering is an answer. Delay is not neutral.

Every ignored warning felt survivable because the walls remained standing. That delay trained them to confuse patience with permission. Survival is not always safety. Continued access is not the same as continued alignment.

Behavior

Hearing the same warning and making no change.

Attitude

Assuming conviction can be postponed.

Root

Self retains the right to decide when God will be obeyed.

Alignment

Answer clearly while mercy is speaking.

Section Two

Jerusalem falls

Lamentations 1:1 · KJV

“How doth the city sit solitary, that was full of people!”

Jeremiah is no longer warning the city about what could happen. He is sitting inside what happened. The crowded city is empty. The influential city is controlled by another kingdom. The things Jerusalem trusted cannot rescue her.

Babylon did not create the nation’s spiritual collapse. Babylon exposed what had already developed within. The people moved away from God internally before they were removed from Jerusalem physically.

Private compromise becomes public culture over time.

What Solomon permitted privately became acceptable publicly. What had been an exception became normal. The decisions we normalize become part of what another generation inherits.

When God is removed from the throne, the throne does not remain empty. Desire, pride, acceptance, relationships, culture, or self takes the seat. Then our decisions serve whatever has become king.

Section Three

Babylon and final judgment

2 Kings 25:8–11 · KJV

Babylon burned the house of the LORD, broke down the walls, and carried the people away.

Babylon gives a visible picture of exile. The people woke under the authority of a kingdom they had been warned was coming. Yet Babylonian captivity ended. A remnant returned. The temple and walls could be rebuilt.

Captivity ends. Final judgment does not.
Revelation 20:11–15 · KJV

The great white throne. The books opened. The lake of fire. The second death.

The issue is not merely whether we want to avoid hell. The deeper question is whether we want the God of heaven. We cannot demand His gifts while refusing His rule. Heaven is not an improved version of a life still governed by self. It is life restored under the uncontested reign of God.

Section Four

A breath of mercy

Lamentations 3:22–23 · KJV

“It is of the LORD’S mercies that we are not consumed… They are new every morning: great is thy faithfulness.”

Jeremiah says this while the city remains in ruins. Mercy does not deny the rubble or erase the seriousness of rebellion. Mercy means the story is not yet closed.

Every morning is an opportunity we did not create. Yet new mercy does not mean unlimited mornings. Another day is not proof that refusal is harmless. It is proof that mercy is still speaking.

Mercy is opportunity to repent, not permission to continue.

Response

Mercy, judgment, and the gospel

Hebrews 9:27 · KJV

“And as it is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment.”

Mercy is available today, but we do not control the number of days we receive. The dangerous lie is not always “God is not real.” The lie is “I have time.” It allows a person to believe the truth while refusing to answer it now.

Acts 2:38 · KJV

“Repent, and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the remission of sins, and ye shall receive the gift of the Holy Ghost.”

Salvation calls for a response. Repentance surrenders our direction. Baptism in the name of Jesus Christ obeys the gospel and joins us to His burial. The Holy Ghost is God dwelling within us, giving power to live under His rule.

Titus 2:11–12 · KJV

Grace teaches us to deny ungodliness and worldly lusts, and to live soberly, righteously, and godly in this present world.

Grace does not preserve our right to remain unchanged. Grace gives forgiveness, power, and opportunity to align with God.

Every ignored warning felt survivable until the day it was not. Mercy gives us today, but mercy was never given so we could postpone obedience.
